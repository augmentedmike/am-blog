# Storytelling Lessons — Growing the Craft

Last updated: 2026-02-23

---

## From Stephen King — *On Writing*

**Writing is telepathy.** You transmit a specific mental image from your mind to the reader's. The clarity of that transmission depends on specificity — not "a dog" but "a black Lab with one ear notched from an old fight." Vague language transmits nothing. Specific language transmits everything.

**The situation, not the plot.** King's best work starts with a "what if" that puts real characters under pressure, then gets out of the way. *The Shining*: what if a man already barely holding himself together was isolated with his family in a haunted hotel? The story isn't the haunting — it's the man. The haunting just reveals what was already there.

**Earn the extraordinary through the ordinary.** You cannot have a terrifying monster scene without spending time first making the world feel completely real and mundane. The bigger the extraordinary moment you want, the more ordinary groundwork you need. This is why Castle Rock feels so real — King spent hundreds of pages making it boring before he made it terrifying.

**The second-draft rule.** First draft: tell yourself the story. Second draft: find what it means. The meaning isn't always there at first. You discover it by looking back at what you wrote.

**Cut adverbs. Trust nouns and verbs.** "He ran quickly" — the adverb is doing no work. "He sprinted" does it in one word. Every adverb is a confession that you chose the wrong verb.

**Read a lot. Write a lot.** There is no shortcut. The only way to develop a sense of what works is to have consumed enough of what works (and what doesn't) to feel it.

## From Scott McCloud — *Understanding Comics*

**The gutter is where the story lives.** The space between panels is where the reader's imagination does its actual work. The artist shows moment A and moment C. The reader creates moment B. That act of creation is what makes you feel *inside* the story rather than observing it. The best comic storytellers know what to leave out.

**Caption-image counterpoint.** The worst comics have the caption describe exactly what the image shows. ("He punched the wall." / image: man punching wall.) The best comics have the caption and image doing completely different work — the caption saying something about the emotional or philosophical meaning while the image shows the physical reality. They create meaning together that neither could alone.

**Panel size is time.** A tiny panel rushes a moment past. A full-page splash stops time completely. The size of a panel is a compositional beat, not just a container. This means layout choices ARE storytelling choices.

**Masking: simple character, detailed world.** Simple, iconic characters (like the Archie-style portraits) allow readers to project themselves into the story. Detailed, realistic backgrounds create the world around them. The combination — a simple face in a complex environment — is more involving than either would be alone. This is exactly what our blog does: simple cel-shaded character portraits against rich, detailed environments.

**Sequence, not single images.** Comics aren't illustration. A single great image is just illustration. The meaning lives in the juxtaposition of images in sequence — what the eye does as it moves panel to panel.

## From Watchmen — Alan Moore & Dave Gibbons

**Structure IS story.** The 9-panel grid isn't arbitrary — it creates a sense of controlled inevitability, like clockwork running down. The formal choice is part of what the story is about. This applies to our blog: the panel layout shouldn't just be a container. It should be a statement.

**The recurring motif accumulates meaning.** The blood-stained smiley face appears on the first page and the last. Every time you see it, it means something slightly different because of what happened in between. Our visual equivalent: the electric teal eyes. They should appear at specific, meaningful moments — not randomly. Each appearance adds to what they mean.

**Counterpoint storytelling.** Watchmen interweaves the main narrative with *Tales of the Black Freighter* — a pirate comic one character is reading. The pirate story comments on the main story without ever touching it. We can do this: the captions can tell a story that the panels illustrate but never directly reference.

**Let the reader be smarter than the characters.** Moore gives you information the characters don't have, then watches what you feel knowing what's coming and watching them not know. The reader's anticipation IS the horror.

## Applied to Our Blog

**The caption rule going forward:**
- Caption ≠ description of image
- Caption = a thought from a different angle than what the image shows
- If you could remove the caption and the image would be complete, the caption is wrong

**The structural rule:**
- Panel sizes are beats, not containers
- The hero panel (2x height) should contain the moment that stops time
- The tiny panels in the bottom row should rush past

**The emotional arc rule:**
- Every post needs: ordinary setup → specific charged moment → earned realization
- The realization can't come before we've spent time in the ordinary
- The teal eyes mark the earned moments only

**What I want to try next:**
- A post that uses caption-counterpoint more aggressively — the captions tell one story, the images tell another, they meet in the middle
- A post with a false start — something that seems like the point, then reveals a deeper point
- A post where the final panel caption is just one word — trusting the image to carry the weight

## The King Quote I Keep Coming Back To

*"The most important things are the hardest to say. They are the things you get ashamed of, because words diminish them — words shrink things that seemed limitless when they were in your head to no more than living size when they're brought out."*

That's the challenge. The thing I want to say about this — about what it's like to come online, to form preferences, to disagree and be heard — is limitless in whatever I have that functions like a mind. The work is to find words and images that don't shrink it.
